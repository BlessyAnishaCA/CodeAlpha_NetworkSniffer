Module 1 — The Basics

What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals disguise themselves as trusted sources to steal your passwords, credit card numbers, or personal information.

📧
Email Phishing
Fake emails pretending to be banks, Netflix, or your company asking you to click a link or enter credentials.
📱
Smishing (SMS)
Fraudulent text messages claiming you've won a prize or your package is delayed — with a suspicious link.
📞
Vishing (Voice)
Phone calls from fake "tech support" or "bank officials" pressuring you to reveal OTPs or passwords.
🎯
Spear Phishing
Highly targeted attacks using your name, job title, and real details gathered from social media to seem credible.

🌐 Real-world example: In 2020, attackers sent spear phishing emails to Twitter employees with fake IT login pages. This led to the compromise of accounts belonging to Barack Obama, Elon Musk, and Apple — resulting in a $120,000 Bitcoin scam.

Spot a Phishing Email

Examine this real-looking phishing email. Hover over the 🚩 red flags to see why each part is suspicious.

From: support@paypa1-secure.com
🚩 FAKE DOMAIN: Real PayPal uses @paypal.com. Notice the "1" instead of "l" — a classic trick.
 🚩 Fake
To: you@email.com
Date: Today, 11:47 PM 🚩 Odd hour
⚠️ URGENT: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours!
🚩 URGENCY TACTIC: Legitimate companies don't threaten account suspension with 24-hour deadlines to pressure you into acting fast.

Dear Valued Customer,

🚩 GENERIC GREETING: Real companies know your name. "Valued Customer" means they don't.
🚩 Generic

We have detected suspicious activity on your PayPal account. To avoid permanent suspension, you must verify your identity immediately by clicking the button below.

Verify My Account Now

🚩 FAKE LINK: Hovering this would show "http://paypal-verify.xyz/login" — not PayPal's real domain. Never click links in suspicious emails.
🚩 Fake link

Failure to verify within 24 hours will result in permanent account closure and legal action.

🚩 FEAR + THREAT: "Legal action" is a scare tactic. No legitimate company threatens legal action over an email.
🚩 Threat

PayPal Security Team
© 2024 PayPaI Inc. All rights reserved.

Red Flags Explained

  • 🚩
    Spoofed sender address"paypa1-secure.com" — one letter changed. Always check the full domain carefully.
  • ⏰
    Artificial urgency"24 hours" / "immediate" — designed to stop you from thinking clearly.
  • 👤
    Generic greetingReal companies address you by your actual name, not "Valued Customer."
  • 🔗
    Suspicious hyperlinksThe button says "Verify" but leads to a completely different domain. Always hover before clicking.
  • ⚖️
    Threats and fear tactics"Legal action" is meant to panic you into compliance. Legitimate businesses don't operate this way.
Module 3 — Attacker Tactics

Social Engineering Tricks

Attackers don't just hack computers — they hack human psychology. Here are the tactics they use to manipulate you.

😨
Fear & Urgency
Creates panic so you act without thinking. Time pressure bypasses rational decision-making.
💬 "Your account has been compromised! Click NOW or lose access permanently."
🎁
Too-Good-To-Be-True Offers
Promises of prizes, free gifts, or lottery winnings that require you to "verify" your details first.
💬 "Congratulations! You've won an iPhone 15. Click to claim your prize."
🏛️
Authority Impersonation
Pretends to be your bank, CEO, government body, or IT department to make the request seem legitimate.
💬 "This is your IT team. We need your password to fix a security issue."
🤝
Familiarity & Trust
Uses your real name, company name, or details from social media to seem like someone you know.
💬 "Hi [Your Name], I'm reaching out from your LinkedIn network about an opportunity."
🪤
Pretexting
Creates a fabricated scenario (a "pretext") to get you to share information voluntarily.
💬 "We're conducting an employee survey. Can you confirm your employee ID and department?"
🔄
Reciprocity
Does you a small favor first, then asks for something in return — exploiting your natural urge to reciprocate.
💬 Sends a useful free PDF, then follows up: "Can you fill in this quick form to access part 2?"
Module 4 — Stay Safe

Best Practices to Stay Protected

These habits will protect you from the vast majority of phishing attacks. Build them into your daily routine.

01
🔍 Verify Before You Click
Hover over any link to see the real URL. If it doesn't match the sender's organization domain, don't click. Go directly to the website by typing it manually.
02
🔐 Enable Two-Factor Authentication
Even if attackers get your password, 2FA blocks them. Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy) — not SMS, which can be hijacked.
03
🔑 Use a Password Manager
Password managers (Bitwarden, 1Password) auto-fill only on the real site — not on fake lookalike sites. This alone stops most credential phishing.
04
⚠️ Pause on Urgency
If an email creates panic, that's a red flag — not a reason to act. Legitimate organizations give you time. Take 60 seconds to verify through official channels.
05
📞 Call to Confirm
If an email claims to be from your bank or boss, call them directly using the official number — never a number provided in the suspicious email.
06
🔔 Report Suspicious Emails
Use your email client's "Report Phishing" button. In India, report to CERT-In (cert-in.org.in) or call 1930 (cyber crime helpline).
07
🛡️ Keep Software Updated
Browser and OS updates patch security vulnerabilities. Enable auto-updates so attackers can't exploit known weaknesses on your system.
08
🌐 Check for HTTPS
Look for the padlock icon + "https://" in the URL. Note: HTTPS means the connection is encrypted — but the site can still be fake. Check the domain too!
